What it treats.

Telogen effluvium: the diffuse hair shed that follows rapid weight loss like clockwork. The body reads rapid weight loss as a stress and pulls follicles out of the active growth phase into the resting phase. Three to six months later, the rested follicles shed in a single wave.

Insurance-claims data on 187,400 GLP-1 users vs matched controls finds a 1.76-fold increase in telogen effluvium and a 1.64-fold increase in androgenetic alopecia. Iron, vitamin D, and zinc deficiencies make the wave larger and the regrowth slower.

Why that matters.

What you find on the pillow and in the brush from month four onward: a noticeable shed that does not slow until you address what triggered it. Biotin is the wrong answer — it has no RCT support in this pattern, and at high doses gives false-high TSH readings on thyroid panels.

The deficiencies driving the shed are the right answer. Roots dosed to move ferritin above 30 and vitamin D above 30, the two thresholds the dermatology literature most consistently associates with telogen effluvium.

How it works.

Four active ingredients, each chosen against a published threshold:

  • Ferrous bisglycinate (iron)
    Chelated, gut-tolerant iron. Dosed to move ferritin above 30 ng/mL — the threshold most consistently associated with telogen effluvium in dermatology cohorts.
  • Vitamin D3
    Cholecalciferol. Dosed to move 25-OH vitamin D above 30 ng/mL, the second threshold from the same literature.
  • Zinc picolinate
    For the structural side of the hair shaft. The published deficiency band for zinc is one of the four that GLP-1s drive.
  • Saw palmetto
    5-alpha reductase modulator. For the androgenetic component the EHR data identifies in 1.64x of GLP-1 users.
  • No biotin
    Deliberately omitted. Zero RCT support in this pattern; interferes with thyroid panels at supplement doses.
