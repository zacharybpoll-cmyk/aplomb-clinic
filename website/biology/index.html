The four concerns. What the drug does, and how often.

GLP-1 receptor agonists do not just suppress appetite. They modulate dermal fibroblast signaling, slow gastric emptying, drive ketosis, and shift oral physiology. Four distinct concerns follow. Each has a measured frequency, a distinct evidence base, and a distinct intervention.

Reviewed by Zachary Poll, Founder of APLOMB. Cambridge, Penn, CHOP. · Last updated May 14, 2026.

1. GLP-1 facial change. Two drivers, not one. Volume below; dermal thinning above.

Onset 9 to 16 weeks. 50 to 100 mL of midface volume goes in the first 90 days of rapid loss (Cotofana 2021).

Two drivers run in parallel during rapid GLP-1 weight loss. First, the volumetric driver: the body draws down its facial fat compartments, like every other fat depot. Studies of midface volume show 50-100 mL disappears across the buccal, malar, and submalar compartments in the first 90 days of significant weight loss (Cotofana 2021). For that layer, fillers and biostimulators are the right intervention.

Second, the dermal driver: caloric restriction drops the protein and cofactor supply that fibroblasts need to maintain collagen, and the dermis itself thins. Skin texture changes; fine-line depth deepens; the appearance of the hollows is amplified by the layer above. Mechanistically the same dermal aging seen in any rapid-weight-loss population (PMC12110338).

The combined effect compresses into a 9-16 week window: hollowed mid-face, deepened nasolabial folds, a softening jawline, and the look of a face that has aged ten years in twelve weeks. For the volumetric half, the right call is a board-certified dermatologist about HA fillers, Sculptra, or fat grafting. Frank, the NYC dermatologist who named the pattern, puts the average reflation spend at $5,000 once the volume is gone (Frank, NYC, via TODAY).

2. Hair loss. Telogen effluvium follows rapid weight loss like clockwork.

1.76x more telogen effluvium in GLP-1 users than matched controls, across 187,400 pairs (Shah 2024).

The body reads rapid weight loss as a stress and pulls follicles out of the active growth (anagen) phase into the resting (telogen) phase. Three to six months later the rested follicles shed in a single wave. Insurance-claims data on 187,400 GLP-1 users matched to 187,400 controls finds a 1.76-fold increase in telogen effluvium and a 1.64-fold increase in androgenetic alopecia (Shah 2024).

Iron, vitamin D, and zinc deficiencies make the wave larger and the regrowth slower. Ferritin under 30 ng/mL and 25-OH vitamin D under 30 ng/mL are the two thresholds most consistently associated with telogen effluvium in dermatology cohorts (Famenini 2014).

Biotin is the wrong answer. It has no RCT support in this pattern. At supplement-level doses it interferes with troponin and TSH assays (FDA Safety Communication 2017), giving false-high readings on thyroid panels, the exact panels women already over-monitor in their forties and fifties.

Aplomb. Roots is dosed to move ferritin and 25-OH vitamin D above the published thresholds, with zinc picolinate for the structural side of the hair shaft and saw palmetto for the androgenetic component the EHR data identifies. No biotin.

3. Nausea. Delayed gastric emptying. The vagal nerve, on alert.

44% reported it in STEP-1; 17 to 22% in SURMOUNT-1. The single largest reason people quit before the dose works.

GLP-1 medications slow gastric emptying. That is the whole point of the drug for blood sugar; it is also the source of the nausea. In the STEP-1 trial, 44% of semaglutide users reported nausea, peaking in weeks one to four of titration and at every dose increase (Wilding 2021). In SURMOUNT-1, tirzepatide nausea ran 17-22% across doses (Jastreboff 2022).

It is the single largest reason people quit before they get to the dose that works. The window is short: week one peaks, week three is hardest, week eight quiets if you titrate properly. The right intervention timed to that window is the difference between staying on long enough for the drug to work and quitting before it does.

Ginger at 1 g/day has the strongest evidence base in adjacent populations, the Cochrane review covers 26 RCTs in pregnancy and chemotherapy nausea, with a 30-50% reduction at this dose. Vitamin B6 (pyridoxine), the same B6 used in pregnancy nausea protocols, adds when ginger plateaus (Matthews 2015). Electrolytes address the dehydration that smaller meals plus occasional vomiting drives faster than most users notice.

4. Bad breath. Hyposalivation, ketosis, gastric stasis.

Roughly 30% of GLP-1 users self-report it (ADA reporting), against a 5 to 10% baseline.

Three converging mechanisms (PMC PMC12729639, 2025). One: GLP-1 medications reduce salivary flow. Saliva is the oral cavity’s primary defense against bacterial overgrowth and the substrate that washes volatile compounds off the tongue and palate. Two: the early-ketosis window of rapid weight loss raises circulating acetone, which is exhaled as part of the fruity-metallic component of the breath. Three: slowed gastric emptying lets fermentation produce volatile sulfur compounds (hydrogen sulfide and methyl mercaptan) that reach the oral cavity through eructation.

ADA reporting puts roughly 30% of Ozempic users self-reporting bad breath, a higher prevalence than the dermatology pattern (5-10%) and similar to the bariatric population. Hershey’s Q1 2026 earnings call attributed +8% Ice Breakers segment growth to GLP-1 user pull-through, the commercial signal that the malodor pattern is real, persistent, and unaddressed by the existing OTC category.

Sugar-free mints do not address salivary flow, acetone, or gastric fermentation. They are a hygiene-category intervention deployed against a physiology problem.
